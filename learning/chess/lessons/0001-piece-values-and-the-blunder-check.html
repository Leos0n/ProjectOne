Piece Values & the Blunder Check

Lesson 0001 · ~15 minutes · Mission: climb your online rating

Below roughly 1200 online, most games are not won — they are lost, by giving pieces away for free. The fastest rating gain available to you is not an opening or a strategy: it is simply stopping the free gifts. This lesson gives you the two tools for that: knowing what each piece is worth, and a 3-second routine to run before every single move.

1. What the pieces are worth

Chess players measure material in points, with the pawn as the unit (Lichess — Learn):

PieceValue
♙ Pawn1
♘ Knight3
♗ Bishop3
♖ Rook5
♕ Queen9
♔ King∞ (lose it, lose the game)

These numbers are how you decide whether a trade is good. Trading your knight (3) for their rook (5) gains you 2 points — that's called winning the exchange. Trading your queen (9) for a knight (3) loses 6 — almost always losing.

2. The Blunder Check

Before every move you make, ask — in this order:

  1. Checks — after my move, can they give check? Is my king safe?
  2. Captures — what can they capture after my move? Especially: does my move leave anything undefended?
  3. Their last move — what did it attack or threaten? Did I answer it?

A piece that can be captured for free (or for something cheaper) is called hanging. The blunder check exists to catch hanging pieces — yours and theirs — before the move is played, not after.

This will feel slow at first. That is the point: effortful checking is what builds the automatic board vision stronger players have. After a few hundred games it runs in under a second without you noticing.

3. Quiz — no peeking back

Recall from memory. Equal-length answers, no formatting clues.

1. You can trade your bishop for their rook. The point swing is…

Bishop = 3, rook = 5. You give 3, you get 5: +2. This is "winning the exchange".

2. They offer to trade their knight for your queen. You should…

Queen 9 vs knight 3 — accepting loses 6 points of material.

3. The first thing to look for in the blunder check is…

Checks first — king safety overrides everything. Then captures, then their last move's threat.

4. A piece that can be taken for free is said to be…

Hanging. Pins and forks are tactics — coming in a future lesson.

4. Practice (this week, ~1 hour total)

  1. Create a free account on lichess.org if you don't have one.
  2. Play 3 games at 15+10 time control (15 min + 10 s/move — slow enough to actually run the blunder check on every move). Your only goal: zero pieces hung. Winning is irrelevant this week.
  3. Do 10 puzzles at lichess.org/training — most beginner puzzles are exactly "spot the hanging piece".
  4. After each game, click "Computer analysis" and count your blunders (red ?? marks). Watch the number drop across the 3 games.

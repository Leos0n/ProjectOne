The Anatomy of a Beautiful Website

Lesson 0001 · ~20 minutes · Mission: image → real implementation

Your entire pipeline — prompting image models, judging mockups, directing Claude Code — runs on one ability: seeing what makes a design good, and naming it. "Make it look better" is not a direction. "Increase the spacing between sections and drop the body text to a muted gray" is. This lesson gives you the four levers that explain about 90% of why a page looks designed or amateur (Refactoring UI).

The Four Levers

1. Hierarchy — what do I look at first?

On a good page your eye lands somewhere deliberate, then flows in order: headline → subline → button. Hierarchy is built by making important things bigger, bolder, darker — and, just as importantly, by making everything else smaller, lighter, and grayer. Amateur pages make everything loud, so nothing is.

2. Spacing — does it have room to breathe?

The most common beginner tell is cramped layouts. Premium sites use far more whitespace than feels natural — around sections, between lines, inside buttons. Related things sit close together; unrelated things sit far apart. When a mockup feels "cheap", the first suspect is always spacing.

3. Typography — do the letters feel intentional?

Good pages use one or two typefaces, a small set of deliberate sizes (a type scale), and comfortable line length (roughly 45–75 characters). Body text is rarely pure black on pure white — it's softened toward gray. Headlines are tight; paragraphs are relaxed.

4. Color — is there discipline?

A designed page commits to a palette: usually one dominant neutral family, one accent color used sparingly (buttons, links, key highlights), and real contrast between text and background. Amateur pages use too many colors at full saturation, all competing.

The critique routine — for any page or mockup, ask in order:

  1. Hierarchy — where does my eye go first? Is that the right place?
  2. Spacing — is anything cramped? Are groups clear?
  3. Typography — how many fonts/sizes? Comfortable to read?
  4. Color — one accent, used with discipline? Enough contrast?

This is to design what the blunder check is to chess: run it on everything — including (especially) AI-generated mockups before you build them.

Quiz — no peeking back

1. Hierarchy is created by emphasizing key elements and also by…

De-emphasizing the rest (smaller, lighter, grayer). If everything shouts, nothing leads.

2. A layout feels "cheap". The first lever to inspect is…

Cramped spacing is the most common amateur tell. Premium = more whitespace than feels natural.

3. A disciplined palette typically means…

Neutrals do most of the work; one accent appears sparingly at moments that matter.

4. Comfortable body-text line length is roughly…

45–75 characters per line. Much longer and the eye loses its place; much shorter and reading feels choppy.

Practice — your first critiques (~30 min, this week)

  1. Pick 3 websites: one you find beautiful, one you find ugly, and one from Awwwards or Land-book.
  2. For each, run the 4-step critique routine and write 3–4 sentences naming specifically what works or fails, lever by lever. Use the critique checklist.
  3. Bring your three critiques back to your teacher (the Claude session on this branch). We'll review them together, sharpen the vocabulary, and that becomes your first learning record.
